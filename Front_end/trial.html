Nature of Stationary Points: Second order taylor formula #

Once again imagine the hillock with a nice flowing river in it - Of course we are dealing with scalar fields. We have with us a critical point.






We want to observe whether this is point is a Maxima, Minima or a Saddle Point.

We can decide this by the sign of \( f(\vec x) - f(\vec a) \) for \(\vec x \) near \(\vec a \) . Look at this equation, the First order taylor formula for derivatives of scalar fields.

\[f(\vec a+ \vec y) - f(\vec a) = \nabla f( \vec a ) . \vec y + \lVert y\rVert\ E(\vec a,\vec y) \]




Substituting \(\nabla f(\vec a) = 0 \) in here, we get

\[f(\vec a+ \vec y) - f(\vec a) = \lVert y\rVert\ E(\vec a,\vec y) \]

As the norm of a vector is always positive, the deciding factor is that error term!

Now it's a matter of computing this term - E

It can be easily computed that by doing by expressing it as the Hessian Matrix

\[\frac 12 \sum\limits_{i = 1}^n \sum\limits_{j = 1}^n {D_{ij}}f(\vec a){a_i} {a_j} \]

Here, the Ds are the partial derivatives. As this is a Quadratic Form, it can be simply written as

\[\frac 12 y^t H(\vec a) y \ \ \ | \ \ \ H(\vec x) = [D_{ij}f(\vec x)] \]

The special form is of most value to us as it provides us a method of easy computation. Once expressed in this form the sign of the term is explained by Diagonalization of Quadratic forms.

  1. Iff all eigenvalues of H(x) are positive, the whole term if positive
  2. Iff all eigenvalues of H(x) are negative, the whole term if negative
Thus applying the same for H(a), we get the required implications
  1. Iff all eigenvalues of H(a) are positive, there is a relative minimum at a
  2. Iff all eigenvalues of H(x) are negative, there is a relative maximum at a
  3. If the eigenvalues are mixed, then H(x) has a saddle point at a
